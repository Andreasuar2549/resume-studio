# Question bank — elicitation techniques

The purpose of every question is to convert an annotation (`[NO METRIC]`, `[VAGUE]`, `[THIN]`) or a JD gap into evidence. Never run a generic questionnaire. Batch 3–5 questions, highest-value first (recent + target-relevant beats ancient history), always with an exit hatch ("good enough, move on" is a valid answer).

## Why draft-first matters

People freeze on "tell me about your impact" but talk fluently when correcting something concrete. Show the annotated draft, then ask. The question should quote the draft line it's trying to improve.

## The decomposition ladder (for any claim)

Push each accomplishment down this ladder until it stops yielding:

1. **Baseline** — what was the number/state before?
2. **Delta** — what changed, by how much?
3. **Timeframe** — over what period?
4. **Attribution** — what was YOUR specific contribution vs the team's? The sharpest form: *"What would have happened if you hadn't been there?"*
5. **Evidence** — where does that number live (dashboard, review, email)? (This sets provenance and pre-empts interview probing.)

## Patterns that surface forgotten material

- **The review mirror:** "What did your manager praise you for in your last performance review?" — surfaces accomplishments people never self-report.
- **The owned number:** "What number were you ultimately on the hook for in that role?" — reveals real scope regardless of title.
- **The crisis probe:** "What broke during your time there, and what did you do?" — incident stories are dense with initiative and judgment.
- **The typical week:** "Walk me through a normal week in that role" — surfaces invisible scope (mentoring, hiring, vendor management) that never makes it into self-descriptions.
- **The proud ramble:** "Talk (or voice-note) for five minutes about the project you're proudest of — don't structure it, just talk." Mine the transcript afterward.
- **The peer lens:** "If your closest colleague described what you were the go-to person for, what would they say?"
- **The quantification rescue** (when they insist something can't be measured): ask *how many, how long, how often, for whom*. "Improved onboarding" → how many new hires, ramp time before vs after, who noticed? A modest real number beats a vague superlative — the act of measuring itself signals rigor.

## Mid-TAILOR questions (partial-evidence gaps)

When the JD demands something the corpus only weakly supports, ask narrowly and cite both sides: "The JD emphasizes enterprise sales experience. The corpus has the Acme upsell project but no deal sizes — do you remember the range, and was the motion self-serve or sales-led?" Answers go into the **corpus** (with provenance "user interview <date>"), not just into this one resume — that's how tailoring sessions compound.

## What NOT to do

- Don't ask what you can look up (their own uploaded documents, public company facts).
- Don't ask more than ~5 questions per round; fatigue kills the whole flow. Multiple short rounds beat one interrogation.
- Don't accept a vague answer twice — rephrase once using a different pattern above, then mark it `[VAGUE]` and move on. The corpus remembers; a future session can retry.
- Don't invent a plausible number to fill a hole the user couldn't fill. Write the bullet without the number, or drop it.
